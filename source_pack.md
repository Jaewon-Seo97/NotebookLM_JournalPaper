## [S[1]] Hernström et al.
- **Full citation:** Veronica Hernström, Viktoria Josefsson, Hanna Sartor, David Schmidt, Anna-Maria Larsson, Solveig Hofvind, Ingvar Andersson, Aldana Rosso, Oskar Hagberg, Kristina Lång. Screening performance and characteristics of breast cancer detected in the Mammography Screening with Artificial Intelligence trial (MASAI): a randomised, controlled, parallelgroup, non-inferiority, single-blinded, screening accuracy study. Lancet Digit Health 2025; 7: e175–83. (S[1], Article Title and Publication Info)
- **Design:** randomised, controlled, parallel-group, non-inferiority, single-blinded, screening-accuracy study (S[1], Methods Study design and participants)
- **Population / Setting:** women eligible for mammography screening within the Swedish national screening programme, recruited at four screening sites in southwest Sweden (Malmö, Lund, Landskrona, and Trelleborg) (S[1], Methods Study design and participants)
- **N (표본 크기):** 105 934 women were randomly assigned; 105 915 women were included in the modified intention-to-treat analysis (53 043 in the intervention group and 52 872 in the control group) (S[1], Results)
- **AI의 역할:** triage screening examinations to single or double reading (based on malignancy risk scores 1–10) and as detection support highlighting suspicious findings (S[1], Summary Methods and Procedures)
- **Primary outcome (지표명):** interval cancer rate (S[1], Outcomes)
- **Primary outcome (수치):** 명시 없음 (will be assessed after all participants have had at least a 2-year follow-up, estimated December, 2024 plus 6 months) (S[1], Outcomes)
- **Secondary outcome (있으면):** Cancer-detection rate: 6·4 per 1000 (95% CI 5·7–7·1) in the intervention group vs 5·0 per 1000 (4·4–5·6) in the control group (ratio 1·29 [95% CI 1·09–1·51; p=0·0021]); Screen-reading workload: 44·2% reduction (S[1], Findings and Table 2)
- **효과의 방향:** 향상 (Increased cancer detection and reduced workload without increasing false positives) (S[1], Interpretation)
- **저자가 명시한 한계:** Generalisability (context of a Swedish screening programme, low baseline recall rates, use of a single mammography and AI vendor); Race and ethnicity were not registered (S[1], Discussion)
- **저자의 결론 (1문장, 원문 기반):** The findings suggest that AI contributes to the early detection of clinically relevant breast cancer and reduces screen-reading workload without increasing false positives. (S[1], Interpretation)
- **Abstract:** Summary Background Emerging evidence suggests that artificial intelligence (AI) can increase cancer detection in mammography screening while reducing screen-reading workload, but further understanding of the clinical impact is needed. Methods In this randomised, controlled, parallel-group, non-inferiority, single-blinded, screening-accuracy study, done within the Swedish national screening programme, women recruited at four screening sites in southwest Sweden (Malmö, Lund, Landskrona, and Trelleborg) who were eligible for mammography screening were randomly allocated (1:1) to AI-supported screening or standard double reading. The AI system (Transpara version 1.7.0 ScreenPoint Medical, Nijmegen, Netherlands) was used to triage screening examinations to single or double reading and as detection support highlighting suspicious findings. This is a protocol-defined analysis of the secondary outcome measures of recall, cancer detection, false-positive rates, positive predictive value of recall, type and stage of cancer detected, and screenreading workload. This trial is registered at ClinicalTrials.gov, NCT04838756 and is closed to accrual. Findings Between April 12, 2021, and Dec 7, 2022, 105 934 women were randomly assigned to the intervention or control group. 19 women were excluded from the analysis. The median age was 53·7 years (IQR 46·5–63·2). AI-supported screening among 53 043 participants resulted in 338 detected cancers and 1110 recalls. Standard screening among 52 872 participants resulted in 262 detected cancers and 1027 recalls. Cancer-detection rates were 6·4 per 1000 (95% CI 5·7–7·1) screened participants in the intervention group and 5·0 per 1000 (4·4–5·6) in the control group, a ratio of 1·29 (95% CI 1·09–1·51; p=0·0021). AI-supported screening resulted in an increased detection of invasive cancers (270 vs 217, a proportion ratio of 1·24 [95% CI 1·04–1·48]), wich were mainly small lymph-node negative cancers (58 more T1, 46 more lymph-node negative, and 21 more non-luminal A). AI-supported screening also resulted in an increased detection of in situ cancers (68 vs 45, a proportion ratio of 1·51 [1·03–2·19]), with about half of the increased detection being high-grade in situ cancer (12 more nuclear grade III, and no increase in nuclear grade I). The recall and false-positive rate were not significantly higher in the intervention group (a ratio of 1·08 [95% CI 0·99–1·17; p=0·084] and 1·01 [0·91–1·11; p=0·92], respectively). The positive predictive value of recall was significantly higher in the intervention group compared with the control group, with a ratio of 1·19 (95% CI 1·04–1·37; p=0·012). There were 61 248 screen readings in the intervention group and 109 692 in the control group, resulting in a 44·2% reduction in the screen-reading workload. Interpretation The findings suggest that AI contributes to the early detection of clinically relevant breast cancer and reduces screen-reading workload without increasing false positives. (S[1], Summary)


## [S[2]] Yu et al.
- **Full citation:** Yu, F., Moehring, A., Banerjee, O., Salz, T., Agarwal, N., & Rajpurkar, P. Heterogeneity and predictors of the effects of AI assistance on radiologists. Nature Medicine 2024; 30: 837–849. (S[2], Reference 38 in Source 6)
- **Design:** large-scale diagnostic study (S[2], Abstract, Results)
- **Population / Setting:** 140 radiologists from large, medium, and small clinical settings, non-clinical settings, academic and non-academic hospitals, teleradiology companies, and the Vinmec health system in Vietnam. (S[2], Methods Dataset specification)
- **N (표본 크기):** 140 radiologists, 324 patient cases, and 15 pathologies (total 5,190 data points used in binscatter analysis). (S[2], Results, Fig. 2)
- **AI의 역할:** provided prediction probabilities on a 0–100 scale for 15 pathologies along with the patient’s chest X-ray and, if applicable, their clinical history. (S[2], Methods Dataset specification)
- **Primary outcome (지표명):** treatment effect, defined as the improvement in absolute error (the difference between the absolute error of a radiologist when unassisted by AI and the absolute error when assisted by AI). (S[2], Methods Calibration and discrimination performance)
- **Primary outcome (수치):** range of treatment effects from −1.295 to 1.440 (interquartile range (IQR), 0.797) across all pathologies; for high-prevalence pathology labels, the largest range extended from −8.914 to 5.563 (IQR, 3.245) for detecting abnormal chest X-rays. (S[2], Results Heterogeneous treatment effects of AI assistance)
- **Secondary outcome (있으면):** Treatment effects on sensitivity averaged from 1.9% to 11.8% (IQR, 1.9%); treatment effects on specificity averaged from −4.0% to 3.1% (IQR, 1.6%). (S[2], Results Heterogeneous treatment effects of AI assistance)
- **효과의 방향:** 혼재 (substantial heterogeneity in the treatment effects of AI assistance among different radiologists). (S[2], Results Heterogeneous treatment effects of AI assistance)
- **저자가 명시한 한계:** temporal trends could not be analyzed due to randomization; AI assistance contained only predicted probabilities without explanations or localization; lack of exploration into the impact of task granularity; simultaneous presentation of all 15 AI predictions made isolating the effect on individual pathologies challenging; difficult to eliminate the confounding factor of the patient case when studying AI accuracy. (S[2], Discussion)
- **저자의 결론 (1문장, 원문 기반):** Our findings highlight the importance of personalized approaches to clinician–AI collaboration and the importance of accurate AI models. (S[2], Abstract)
- **Abstract:** The integration of artificial intelligence (AI) in medical image interpretation requires effective collaboration between clinicians and AI algorithms. Although previous studies demonstrated the potential of AI assistance in improving overall clinician performance, the individual impact on clinicians remains unclear. This large-scale study examined the heterogeneous effects of AI assistance on 140 radiologists across 15 chest X-ray diagnostic tasks and identified predictors of these effects. Surprisingly, conventional experience-based factors, such as years of experience, subspecialty and familiarity with AI tools, fail to reliably predict the impact of AI assistance. Additionally, lower-performing radiologists do not consistently benefit more from AI assistance, challenging prevailing assumptions. Instead, we found that the occurrence of AI errors strongly influences treatment outcomes, with inaccurate AI predictions adversely affecting radiologist performance on the aggregate of all pathologies and on half of the individual pathologies investigated. Our findings highlight the importance of personalized approaches to clinician–AI collaboration and the importance of accurate AI models. By understanding the factors that shape the effectiveness of AI assistance, this study provides valuable insights for targeted implementation of AI, enabling maximum benefits for individual clinicians in clinical practice. (S[2], Abstract)

## [S[3]] Savardi et al.
- **Full citation**: Savardi, M., Signoroni, A., Benini, S., Vaccher, F., Alberti, M., Ciolli, P., Di Meo, N., Falcone, T., Ramanzin, M., Romano, B., Sozzi, F. & Farina, D. Upskilling or deskilling? Measurable role of an AI-supported training for radiology residents: a lesson from the pandemic. Insights into Imaging (2025) 16:23. (S[3], Article Title)
- **Design**: pilot study (S[3], Abstract)
- **Population / Setting: eight radiology residents (four in their first year and four in their third year) at Radiology Unit 2 of Azienda Socio Sanitaria Territoriale ‘Spedali Civili di Brescia,’ Italy. (S[3], Materials and methods)
- **N (표본 크기)**: 8 residents; 150 chest radiographs (CXRs) (S[3], Abstract, Materials and methods)
- **AI의 역할**: assessment of a multi-regional severity score (Brixia score) of lung compromise in patients affected by COVID-19, integrated into RIS/PACS with confidence levels and explainability maps. (S[3], Abstract, Materials and methods)
- **Primary outcome (지표명): average scoring errors (MAE and SD) and inter-rater agreement (intraclass correlation coefficient, ICC-1). (S[3], Metrics)
- **Primary outcome (수치)**: ICC-1 rose from 0.665 [95% CI 0.57–0.76] for the no-AI scenario, to 0.788 [95% CI 0.71–0.86] for the on-demand-AI case, and reached 0.813 [95% CI 0.74–0.87] in the integrated-AI one. (S[3], Table 4)
- **Secondary outcome (있으면)**: Usage of on-demand-AI: support requested in 70% of cases (S[3], Results); Resilience to machine failure: for AI error levels above 3, trainees maintained an average absolute error of 2.88 (on-demand-AI) and 2.75 (integrated-AI). (S[3], Results)
- **효과의 방향**: 향상 (AI support significantly reduced severity score errors and increased inter-rater agreement by 22%). (S[3], Abstract)
- **저자가 명시한 한계**: pilot study (small sample size); lack of exploration of AI tools’ long-term threats such as satisfaction-of-search effect or diminishing diagnostic skills; lack of quantitative studies in residency training field. (S[3], Introduction, Discussion)
- **저자의 결론 (1문장, 원문 기반)**: With this work, we gathered quantitative and qualitative evidence of the beneficial use of a highperformance AI tool that is well integrated into the diagnostic workflow as a training aid for radiology residents. (S[3], Conclusion)
- **Abstract**: Objectives This article aims to evaluate the use and effects of an artificial intelligence system supporting a critical diagnostic task during radiology resident training, addressing a research gap in this field. Materials and methods We involved eight residents evaluating 150 CXRs in three scenarios: no AI, on-demand AI, and integrated-AI. The considered task was the assessment of a multi-regional severity score of lung compromise in patients affected by COVID-19. The chosen artificial intelligence tool, fully integrated in the RIS/PACS, demonstrated superior performance in scoring compared to the average radiologist. Using quantitative metrics and questionnaires, we measured the ‘upskilling’ effects of using AI support and residents’ resilience to ‘deskilling,’ i.e., their ability to overcome AI errors. Results Residents required AI in 70% of cases when left free to choose. AI support significantly reduced severity score errors and increased inter-rater agreement by 22%. Residents were resilient to AI errors above an acceptability threshold. Questionnaires indicated high tool usefulness, reliability, and explainability, with a preference for collaborative AI scenarios. Conclusion With this work, we gathered quantitative and qualitative evidence of the beneficial use of a highperformance AI tool that is well integrated into the diagnostic workflow as a training aid for radiology residents. (S[3], Abstract)

## [S[4]] Siepmann et al.
- **Full citation**: Siepmann et al. European Radiology (2024) 34:6652–6666 https://doi.org/10.1007/s00330-024-10727-2 (S[4], Header)
- **Design**: retrospective intra-individual comparative reader study (S[4], Materials & methods Study design and dataset characteristics)
- **Population / Setting**: six clinical resident radiologists with varying experience levels (limited, moderate, advanced) at a tertiary academic medical center (University Hospital Aachen, Aachen, Germany). (S[4], Materials & methods Selection of imaging studies, Experimental setup and data collection)
- **N (표본 크기)**: 6 radiologists; 40 selected imaging studies (10 radiographs, 10 CT, 10 MRI, and 10 angiographic studies), resulting in 240 readings unassisted and 240 readings AI-assisted. (S[4], Abstract, Materials and methods)
- **AI의 역할**: "reading room assistance" provided by GPT-4 (OpenAI) to assist in diagnostic decision-making, from differential diagnoses and associated imaging findings to radiologic signs, gradings, and classifications. (S[4], Introduction, Experimental setup and data collection)
- **Primary outcome (지표명)**: diagnostic accuracy (assessed as ‘top-3 performance’ and ‘top-1 performance’) and diagnostic confidence. (S[4], Performance, User experience)
- **Primary outcome (수치)**: top-3 performance: improved from 181/240 (75.4%, unassisted) to 188/240 (78.3%, AI-assisted) [p= 0.130; Cohen’s h: 0.069]; top-1 performance: improved from 154/240 (64.2%, unassisted) to 163/240 (67.9%, AI-assisted) [p= 0.083; Cohen’s h: 0.079]. (S[4], Results)
- **Secondary outcome (있으면)**: diagnostic confidence: significantly higher when readings were AI-assisted (p < 0.001); Hallucinations: 23 responses (7.4%) were classified as hallucinations. (S[4], Results, Abstract)
- **효과의 방향**: 향상 (Integrating GPT-4 improved diagnostic accuracy slightly and diagnostic confidence significantly). (S[4], Conclusion)
- **저자가 명시한 한계**: small sample size per modality (n=10); prohibition of external references (books/online) not reflecting real-world practice; no washout period between sessions; memory retention of studies; evaluation limited to May 2023 version of ChatGPT. (S[4], Discussion Limitations)
- **저자의 결론 (1문장, 원문 기반)**: GPT-4 is a clinically useful adjunct tool that improves diagnostic accuracy slightly and diagnostic confidence significantly, and may partially mitigate the experience gap in radiologists. (S[4], Conclusion)
- **Abstract**: Objectives Large language models (LLMs) have shown potential in radiology, but their ability to aid radiologists in interpreting imaging studies remains unexplored. We investigated the effects of a state-of-the-art LLM (GPT-4) on the radiologists’ diagnostic workflow. Materials and methods In this retrospective study, six radiologists of different experience levels read 40 selected radiographic [n= 10], CT [n= 10], MRI [n= 10], and angiographic [n= 10] studies unassisted (session one) and assisted by GPT-4 (session two). Each imaging study was presented with demographic data, the chief complaint, and associated symptoms, and diagnoses were registered using an online survey tool. The impact of Artificial Intelligence (AI) on diagnostic accuracy, confidence, user experience, input prompts, and generated responses was assessed. False information was registered. Linear mixed-effect models were used to quantify the factors (fixed: experience, modality, AI assistance; random: radiologist) influencing diagnostic accuracy and confidence. Results When assessing if the correct diagnosis was among the top-3 differential diagnoses, diagnostic accuracy improved slightly from 181/240 (75.4%, unassisted) to 188/240 (78.3%, AI-assisted). Similar improvements were found when only the top differential diagnosis was considered. AI assistance was used in 77.5% of the readings. Three hundred nine prompts were generated, primarily involving differential diagnoses (59.1%) and imaging features of specific conditions (27.5%). Diagnostic confidence was significantly higher when readings were AI-assisted (p > 0.001). Twenty-three responses (7.4%) were classified as hallucinations, while two (0.6%) were misinterpretations. Conclusion Integrating GPT-4 in the diagnostic process improved diagnostic accuracy slightly and diagnostic confidence significantly. Potentially harmful hallucinations and misinterpretations call for caution and highlight the need for further safeguarding measures. (S[4], Abstract)

## [S[5]] Heudel et al.
- **Full citation**: Pierre E. Heudel, et al. Artificial intelligence in medicine: a scoping review of the risk of deskilling and loss of expertise among physicians. ESMO Real World Data and Digital Oncology (2026). (S[5], Header)
- **Design**: narrative review of empirical studies, randomized trials, and theoretical analyses / scoping review (S[5], Materials and methods, Results)
- **Population / Setting**: physicians and trainees in clinical practice, particularly in radiology, pathology, endoscopy, and decision support (S[5], Background)
- **N (표본 크기)**: 12 studies included in qualitative synthesis (S[5], Figure 1)
- **AI의 역할**: systems increasingly deployed in clinical practice to improve efficiency and accuracy in diagnostics and decision support (S[5], Background)
- **Primary outcome (지표명)**: risk of deskilling—the erosion of physicians’ expertise due to reliance on automation (S[5], Background)
- **Primary outcome (수치)**: 명시 없음 (as a scoping review, it synthesizes findings from multiple studies rather than reporting a single primary numerical outcome) (S[5], Results)
- **Secondary outcome (있으면)**: automation bias, cognitive offloading, decreased independent diagnostic reasoning, and structural changes in training environments (S[5], Results, Discussion)
- **효과의 방향**: 혼재 (While AI tools improve efficiency and accuracy, empirical studies consistently demonstrate that AI can inadvertently impair physicians’ performance or reduce opportunities for skill maintenance) (S[5], Background, Conclusions)
- **저자가 명시한 한계**: evidence remains fragmented across specialties with heterogeneous study designs; many reports are based on expert opinion or small-scale investigations; conclusion validity for next-generation AI systems; lack of a unified framework for evaluating skill erosion across diverse disciplines (S[5], Discussion)
- **저자의 결론 (1문장, 원문 기반)**: Safeguarding clinical expertise should be considered a central component of AI safety and resilience in medicine. (S[5], Conclusions)
- **Abstract**: Background: Artificial intelligence (AI) systems are increasingly deployed in clinical practice, particularly in radiology, pathology, endoscopy, and decision support. While these tools improve efficiency and accuracy, concerns have arisen about deskilling—the erosion of physicians’ expertise due to reliance on automation. Materials and methods: We conducted a narrative review of empirical studies, randomized trials, and theoretical analyses published up to August 2025. The focus was on quantitative evidence of decreased performance following AI exposure, automation bias, and structural changes in training environments. Sources included PubMed, Embase, and gray literature. Results: Evidence of clinical deskilling, though scarce, is consistent across specialties. In a multicenter randomized trial in colonoscopy, the adenoma detection rate (ADR) dropped significantly from 28.4% to 22.4% when endoscopists reverted to non-AI procedures after repeated AI use, while ADR remained stable with AI assistance (25.3%). In radiology, a controlled study of 27 breast imaging radiologists showed that erroneous AI prompts increased false-positive recalls by up to 12%, even among experienced readers. In computational pathology, experimental web-based tasks revealed that over 30% of participants reversed correct initial diagnoses when exposed to incorrect AI suggestions under time constraints. Structural deskilling has been reported in cytology following the UK’s transition to human papillomavirus primary screening, leading to an 80%-85% reduction in case volumes and consolidation of laboratories from 45 to 8 centers, with major implications for training capacity. Across domains, analyses confirm the presence of automation bias and highlight risks of diminished independent diagnostic reasoning. Conclusions: Although limited in number, empirical studies consistently demonstrate that AI can inadvertently impair physicians’ performance or reduce opportunities for skill maintenance. Quantitative evidence of decreased diagnostic accuracy, error propagation, and training erosion underscores the need for longitudinal monitoring, adaptive curricula, and regulatory frameworks to mitigate deskilling. Safeguarding clinical expertise should be considered a central component of AI safety and resilience in medicine. Key words: artificial intelligence, clinical deskilling, automation bias, medical expertise erosion (S[5], Abstract)

## [S[6]] Lawrence et al.
- **Full citation**: Rachel Lawrence, Emma Dodsworth, Efthalia Massou, et al. Artificial intelligence for diagnostics in radiology practice: a rapid systematic scoping review. eClinicalMedicine 2025;83: 103228. (S[6], Header)
- **Design**: rapid systematic scoping review (S[6], Summary Methods)
- **Population / Setting**: radiology staff, wider clinical staff, residents/students/trainees, patients, and members of the public; global setting covering varied imaging modalities and patient pathways (S[6], Results Table 1)
- **N (표본 크기)**: 140 included studies (identified from 8013 articles) (S[6], Summary Methods)
- **AI의 역할**: support tool for human decision-making in radiologic image interpretation and diagnosis (S[6], Methods Search strategy and selection criteria)
- **Primary outcome (지표명)**: evidence on the use of AI to support diagnostics in radiology, including implementation, experiences, perceptions, quantitative, and cost outcomes (S[6], Summary Background)
- **Primary outcome (수치)**: 명시 없음 (S[6], Results)
- **Secondary outcome (있으면)**: sensitivity (improved in 19/25 studies), specificity (improved in 14/23 studies), image interpretation time (reduction in 13/18 studies), reporting time (reduction in 5/10 studies), and cost-effectiveness (5/6 papers demonstrated monetary benefits) (S[6], Summary Findings and Tables 3-4)
- **효과의 방향**: 혼재 (Evidence demonstrated improvements in diagnostic accuracy and reductions in interpretation time, but findings were mixed regarding experiences, false positives, and the wider impact on workflow efficiency and cost-effectiveness) (S[6], Summary Findings)
- **저자가 명시한 한계**: difficulty in generalising findings across different health systems; only papers in English were included; risk of publication bias in AI research; rapid evolution of the field may have missed very recent evidence; stakeholder workshops only reflected the English NHS context (S[6], Discussion)
- **저자의 결론 (1문장, 원문 기반)**: This will help to draw conclusions about how best to use AI as a complementary tool in the complexities of radiology practice. (S[6], Conclusion)
- **Abstract**: Summary Background The aim of this review was to evaluate evidence on the use of Artificial Intelligence (AI) to support diagnostics in radiology, including implementation, experiences, perceptions, quantitative, and cost outcomes. Methods We conducted a systematic scoping review (PROSPERO registration: CRD42024537518) and discussed emerging findings with relevant stakeholders (radiology staff, public members) using workshops. We searched four databases and the grey literature for articles published between 1st January 2020 and 31st January 2025. Articles were screened for eligibility (N = 8013), resulting in 140 included studies. Studies evaluated implementation (N = 7), perceptions (N = 74), experiences (N = 14), effectiveness (N = 53), and cost (N = 6). Findings Factors influencing AI adoption were identified, including the high technical demand, lack of guidance, training/knowledge, transparency, and expert engagement. Evidence demonstrated improvements in diagnostic accuracy and reductions in interpretation time. However, evidence was mixed regarding experiences of using AI, the risk of increasing false positives, and the wider impact of AI on workflow efficiency and cost-effectiveness. Interpretation The potential benefits of AI are evident, but there is a paucity of evidence in real-world settings, supporting cautiousness in how AI is perceived (e.g., as a complementary tool, not a solution). We outline wider implications for policy and practice and summarise evidence gaps. (S[6], Summary)
